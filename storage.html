HISTORY

Paper tape

Experiment data was punched into rolls of paper with this machine, an were then fed into computers to read and process the code. These were used at the time of the Ferranti Mercury and the IBM very early mainframes.

Punched cards and IBM3000 Information Recorder

German-American statistician Hollerith invented the recording of data on a medium that could then be read by a machine, developing punched card data processing technology for the 1890 U.S. census. He git the idea by looking at a train ticket controller checking tickets. In 1924, he founded International Business Machines Corporation (IBM). Punched cards are therefore older than computers! The first punch cards format had 80 columns and 12 rows. Columns 1 to 5, for example, were the label field, while 73 to 80 were left for comments. If we were to save 1 TB of data (the capacity of your own personal computer, more or less) on punched cards, we would have to tie them together in a line long enough to go around the whole world's conference for 64 times!

Magnetic storage - tapes

Magnetic recording was invented by Danish engineer V. Poulsen in 1900, and it started being used world-wide as a data a data-storage medium in the 50s. The basic principle remains the same, irrespective of the year of manufacture; magnetic tapes are still used at CERN as the main long-term storage medium. The tape is covered with a magnetic pigment of ferromagnetic material like iron. Ferromagnets consist of thousands of microscopic regions of uniform magnetic fields called domains. At the experiments' site at the time of the PS, data was recorded by passing an electrical signal close to the tape. Due to Faraday's law, electrical current induces a magnetic field, which makes the ferromagnetic domains align in one direction. Each domain alignment represents a 1 or a 0 on the tape, making it possible to record information digitally in the form of bits. The tapes were initially carried around by bike and brought to the Data Centre, where operators would attach them to the computer; they were an inseparable part of the computer. The data density a magnetic tape can holdis measured in BPI (bits per inch): in the 60s, most tapes were 200, 556 and 800 bpi. With the arrival of the CDC 7600 mainframe in 1974, tapes with 1600 and 6250 BPI were introduced, with size up to 1 km in length.

Magnetic storage - automated IBM 3851 Mass Storage Cartridges

These beehive structures hosted magnetic bands, which were retrieved and unrolled to read the data by the first automated system, that comprised a robotic arm that was pinching them. Each cylinder has a capacity of 50MB; as they always worked in pair, one mass storage volume corresponded to 100 MB.

Magnetic storagse - tape cartidges

This is a StorageTek RedWood SD-3 tape drive from 1994 (which was sold at a price of $200 in 1994), and next to it the modern IBM cartridge mounting robot. Robotic tape libraries are used together with this helical scan recording drive, which sweeps across the tape every few micrometers with a rotating head. Many industries - from video, to satellite to seismic data - use magnetic tapes to store and back up data. Information retrieval is quite slow and inherently sequential, as the whole tape needs to be windedbefore finding the desired section to be read; however, it is to the present the most reliable and cheapest technology. The tape cartridges evolved from a capacity of 10, 25, 50 Gb. In the 2021 report, 32244 were reported to be in the Data Centre, with about 380 PB used.

Magnetic Hard Disk Drives

From a magnetic disk drive from 1974 (10 MB) to one from 1985 (200 MB), to one from nowadays (5 million MB). The technology is the same, the only difference is the size and capacity. An aaluminium platter (disk) with magnetized coating (ferromagnet). Coated on both sides, so two surfaces contain data. A head places above the disk contains an induction coil which is used to read and write, while the platter spins (induction is the process where a moving magnetic field can create a current in a wire). The head floats a few microns above the surface of the disk - it never comes into direct contact.

Solid State Drives

The elements do not rotate like in HDDs, but are semiconductor memory chips, making SSD’s faster and more robust

Databases

A database is a way to organise and read data, allowing concurrent acess of multiple users. At the time of punch cards, IBM mainframes had extension machines to organise the cards; this could be tought of as the earliest kind of databases. In the 1980s, relational databases became popular, introducing the concet of rows and columns. In the 1990s, object-oriented databasescame about. Today, cloud databases hosted on cloud platforms are widely used, and the CERN Data Centre provides experiments with the database that best suits their needs.

Storage and File System Technologies
